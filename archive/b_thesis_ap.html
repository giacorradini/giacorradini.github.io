The Demographic Transition in Modern Europe: A Comparative Study of England, Wales and France

Gianmarco Corradini, 2026

More than ten years ago, I wrote my bachelor’s thesis on the demographic transition in modern Europe. The thesis compared the demographic histories of England and Wales with that of France, concentrating on the decline of mortality, economic modernization, secularization, and the diffusion of fertility control. It was a literature-based thesis: I reviewed existing explanations and historical evidence, but I did not conduct an original statistical analysis or develop an independent theoretical interpretation.

Looking back, I still consider the historical evidence presented in that thesis valuable. However, I now interpret it through a different theoretical framework. In particular, I believe that dual inheritance theory provides a more coherent explanation of the demographic transition than approaches based exclusively on economic incentives, mortality decline, or individual reproductive choice.

My present argument is that the demographic transition was fundamentally a process of cultural evolution. Economic and epidemiological changes created the conditions within which fertility could decline, but they do not fully explain why low-fertility behaviour emerged, spread across populations, and eventually became dominant. The decisive transformation concerned not only the material conditions of family life, but also the mechanisms through which people acquired values, aspirations, and models of successful behaviour.

The limits of conventional explanations

The classical theory of demographic transition describes a movement from a demographic regime characterized by high mortality and high fertility to one characterized by low mortality and low fertility. Mortality normally declines first as nutrition, sanitation, medicine, and public health improve. Fertility then declines after a period of rapid population growth.

This description identifies an important historical sequence, but it does not by itself explain the decision to restrict fertility. A fall in infant mortality may reduce the number of births required to achieve a desired number of surviving children, but it does not determine what that desired number should be. Nor does it explain why fertility sometimes remained high for decades after mortality had already declined.

Economic theories provide a more explicit mechanism. Industrialization reduced the productive value of children, increased the importance of education, and raised the direct and indirect costs of childrearing. Children became less valuable as workers and more expensive as recipients of parental investment. At the same time, paid employment and professional advancement increased the opportunity cost of parenthood, particularly for women.

These arguments are persuasive, but they normally treat preferences as relatively stable. Individuals are assumed to continue pursuing the same objectives while adjusting their reproductive decisions to new costs, benefits, and constraints. Fertility declines because the economic environment changes.

The historical evidence, however, suggests that modernization also changed the preferences themselves. Ideas about marriage, parenthood, sexuality, personal fulfilment, consumption, and social status did not remain fixed while economic circumstances changed around them. They evolved and spread through increasingly powerful systems of cultural transmission.

The inconclusive legacy of the Princeton European Fertility Project

The Princeton European Fertility Project was one of the most ambitious attempts to study the historical decline of fertility. It reconstructed fertility patterns across hundreds of European administrative regions and documented substantial variation in the timing and speed of the transition.

Its results weakened simple economic accounts. Fertility decline did not consistently begin in the most industrialized regions. France experienced a remarkably early decline, although its industrial development was slower than that of Britain. Germany contained regions that maintained high fertility long after economic modernization had begun. Similar economic transformations therefore produced different demographic outcomes.

At the same time, fertility decline frequently followed linguistic, religious, and cultural boundaries. French-speaking and Flemish-speaking regions of Belgium entered the transition at different times. Catalonia preceded much of the rest of Spain. Brittany and Normandy differed from other parts of France. These patterns indicated that fertility behaviour spread through culturally structured populations rather than simply following income, industrial employment, or urbanization.

In this sense, the Princeton project was both extremely important and theoretically inconclusive. It demonstrated that economic modernization alone could not explain the European fertility transition, but it did not collect the type of evidence required to identify the mechanisms through which culture influenced reproductive behaviour.

The project showed that culture mattered, but it did not possess a theory explaining how cultural traits are transmitted, why some ideas spread more effectively than others, or how cultural evolution can produce behaviour that conflicts with reproductive success. Dual inheritance theory provides a framework for addressing these questions.

Demographic transition as cultural evolution

Dual inheritance theory begins from the idea that human behaviour is shaped by two interacting systems of inheritance: genetic inheritance and cultural inheritance. Culture is not merely a collection of ideas placed on top of an otherwise complete biological organism. It is an inheritance system through which beliefs, practices, skills, preferences, and institutions are transmitted between individuals and across generations.

Genetic and cultural evolution do not necessarily favour the same outcomes. Genetic selection favours traits that increase biological reproduction. Cultural selection favours traits that are successfully learned, imitated, remembered, taught, or institutionally reproduced. A cultural trait can therefore spread even when it reduces the number of children produced by the individuals who adopt it.

In traditional agrarian societies, cultural and genetic interests were often closely aligned. The family was the principal unit of production, consumption, protection, and socialization. Children learned primarily from parents and relatives. Social status was strongly connected to family, lineage, land, and descendants. Under these conditions, cultural norms often promoted marriage, parenthood, family continuity, and the accumulation of resources for future generations.

Modernization disrupted this alignment. Education, urbanization, bureaucratic employment, industrial production, mass literacy, and mass communication increased the importance of cultural influences originating outside the family. Teachers, employers, professional colleagues, politicians, writers, entertainers, and other prestigious individuals became increasingly important cultural models.

The demographic transition should therefore be understood partly as a transition from predominantly vertical cultural transmission, from parents to children, toward more horizontal and oblique forms of transmission, through peers, institutions, prestigious individuals, and media.

Prestige and the diffusion of low fertility

Humans do not evaluate every possible behaviour independently. We frequently use social information, and one of the most important shortcuts is prestige bias: the tendency to learn from people who appear successful, respected, knowledgeable, or socially influential.

Modern societies created new forms of prestige. High status became increasingly associated with educational achievement, professional specialization, career advancement, urban lifestyles, cultural sophistication, and individual autonomy. Success in these domains often required delayed marriage, geographical mobility, long periods of education, and substantial investment in professional competition.

These strategies were frequently associated with smaller families. The people who achieved the greatest visibility and prestige within modern institutions were therefore disproportionately likely to model lifestyles characterized by delayed parenthood and restricted fertility.

Other individuals did not necessarily imitate only their reproductive behaviour. They imitated a broader package of values and aspirations: education, occupational success, consumption, independence, and personal development. Low fertility was transmitted as part of this culturally attractive package.

From this perspective, economic modernization affected fertility indirectly. It created institutions in which low-fertility individuals could acquire prestige and cultural influence. The crucial causal chain was not simply that industrialization made children more expensive. It was also that industrialization reorganized prestige, social learning, and the transmission of aspirations.

Why France moved first

The contrast between France and England and Wales is especially revealing. England experienced earlier and more intense industrialization, but France entered the fertility transition sooner. A strictly economic model would have difficulty explaining this sequence.

France, however, underwent a profound transformation of its social and ideological order. The Revolution weakened traditional authorities, challenged the political and cultural power of the Church, promoted legal equality, and strengthened individualistic conceptions of property and family interest. The Napoleonic inheritance system also encouraged the division of property among heirs, increasing the perceived cost of large families among property-owning households.

These developments did more than alter economic calculations. They changed the cultural legitimacy of fertility control. Deliberately limiting births became increasingly compatible with conceptions of rational household management, individual autonomy, and the preservation of family property.

France can therefore be interpreted as a case in which social and cultural modernization moved ahead of industrial modernization. England and Wales, by contrast, industrialized earlier while maintaining reproductive norms that remained more resistant to deliberate fertility limitation for a longer period.

The different trajectories of the two countries do not demonstrate that economics was irrelevant. They show that economic change was filtered through historically specific cultural systems. Similar material pressures could produce different demographic outcomes because populations differed in their inherited beliefs, institutions, social networks, and sources of prestige.

Culture can be maladaptive

The most important implication of dual inheritance theory is that culture need not maximize biological fitness. This point distinguishes a cultural evolutionary explanation from theories that interpret low fertility as an adaptive response to modern conditions.

One possible adaptive explanation is the trade-off between the quantity and quality of children. Parents may have fewer children because each child requires greater investment in education, health, and social development. Producing fewer, more successful children could theoretically lead to a larger number of surviving descendants in later generations.

This reasoning may explain some reduction in fertility, but it becomes less convincing when fertility falls below replacement level. A reproductive strategy that systematically produces too few descendants to replace the parental generation cannot easily be described as biologically adaptive.

Dual inheritance theory does not require individuals to consciously reject reproduction. Nor does it imply that low-fertility people are irrational. People may act coherently according to culturally acquired preferences. They may pursue education, professional recognition, consumption, independence, leisure, or personal fulfilment. These goals can be entirely rational from the perspective of the individual while still producing a decline in long-term genetic fitness.

Cultural success and reproductive success are therefore different phenomena. A lifestyle can be highly visible, prestigious, and widely imitated while reducing the number of descendants left by its adopters. Culture is capable of producing successful cultural variants that are biologically maladaptive.

Consumerism and competing aspirations

Modern economic development enormously expanded the range of activities competing with parenthood. Individuals could invest time and resources in education, careers, travel, hobbies, entertainment, consumption, and personal relationships outside the extended family.

These alternatives did not merely raise the financial cost of children. They changed the perceived meaning of a successful life. Parenthood ceased to be the only widely available route to adulthood, status, identity, and social recognition.

Consumer culture is particularly important because it continually generates new desires. Rising income does not necessarily lead people to have more children once basic needs are satisfied. It can instead increase the number of goods, experiences, and lifestyles against which parenthood must compete.

The relevant scarcity is therefore not only money. It is also time, attention, identity, and commitment. Even affluent individuals may perceive children as incompatible with the lifestyle they have learned to value.

The transformation of women’s social roles

The expansion of female education was one of the most powerful elements of the demographic transition. Traditional societies generally restricted women’s access to prestigious roles outside the household. Motherhood and domestic competence were consequently central sources of status and identity.

Mass education exposed girls and women to alternative models of success. Schools transmitted knowledge, but they also transmitted aspirations. Educational achievement demonstrated that women could compete successfully for occupational and professional positions previously reserved for men.

As women gained access to these roles, the opportunity costs of early and repeated childbearing increased. More importantly, the cultural definition of female success changed. Marriage and motherhood became possible elements of a successful life rather than its exclusive foundation.

This development cannot be reduced to a mechanical calculation of wages and childcare costs. It represents a transformation in identity, prestige, and socially transmitted preferences. Female education affected fertility because it reorganized both economic opportunities and the cultural models available to women.

Mass communication and accelerating transitions

The European demographic transition developed through schools, cities, migration, political movements, religious change, newspapers, and interpersonal networks. Later transitions occurred in a world with radio, television, cinema, international organizations, and eventually digital media.

These communication technologies increased the speed at which fertility norms could spread. Populations no longer needed to reach the same level of industrial development experienced by nineteenth-century Europe before encountering modern models of family life.

Media frequently portray attractive and prestigious individuals living urban, consumption-oriented lives in which children are absent, delayed, or few. Even without explicitly promoting fertility limitation, such representations communicate assumptions about what modernity and success look like.

The demographic transition can therefore begin at lower levels of economic development when communities become connected to national and global networks of cultural transmission. Ideas about contraception and family size spread not only because they are materially useful, but because they are associated with socially desirable identities.

Resistance as a natural experiment

High-fertility religious communities provide an important test of this interpretation. Groups such as the Amish, Hutterites, and some ultra-Orthodox Jewish communities participate in modern economies and benefit from modern medicine, but have historically maintained much higher fertility than surrounding populations.

Their behaviour cannot be explained simply by poverty, lack of access to contraception, or economic underdevelopment. Their relatively high fertility is sustained by cultural institutions that protect pronatalist norms.

These communities limit exposure to external media, maintain strong family structures, regulate education, create internal systems of prestige, and impose social costs on deviation. Individuals receive much of their cultural information from parents, relatives, religious authorities, and community members rather than from the prestige hierarchy of the wider society.

Such groups are not outside modernity in an economic sense. Their resistance is primarily cultural. They demonstrate that industrial production, material prosperity, and low mortality do not automatically generate low fertility.

Their existence supports the argument that the demographic transition depends on specific mechanisms of cultural transmission. Where communities successfully restrict the spread of external fertility-reducing norms, high fertility can persist despite modernization.

Reinterpreting the demographic transition

I would now reinterpret the demographic transition as the result of an interaction between material change and cultural evolution. Improvements in survival, industrialization, contraception, education, and urbanization were indispensable, but none of them operated independently of culture.

Mortality decline altered the demographic environment. Industrialization changed the economic role of children. Education expanded individual opportunities. Contraception made deliberate fertility control more reliable. Urbanization weakened traditional family structures. Mass communication increased exposure to alternative lifestyles.

Together, these changes transformed the cultural transmission system. Non-parental influences became stronger, prestigious social models became less family-centred, and reproductive behaviour became increasingly detached from the norms favoured by vertical transmission.

Low fertility spread because it was embedded in a broader cultural complex associated with modern success. This complex included education, individualism, professional ambition, consumption, autonomy, and the possibility of constructing an identity outside parenthood.

The demographic transition was therefore not simply a rational adjustment to new economic conditions. It was also the evolutionary diffusion of a new conception of the good life.

Conclusion

My original thesis concluded that the demographic transition resulted from the interaction of economic, epidemiological, and cultural forces. I still agree with that conclusion, but I would now assign culture a more central explanatory role.

Economic and epidemiological modernization changed the environment in which reproductive decisions were made. Dual inheritance theory explains how these transformations altered the transmission and selection of the preferences guiding those decisions.

The evidence collected by the Princeton European Fertility Project was inconclusive in the sense that it undermined simple economic explanations without providing a complete alternative mechanism. Its regional patterns, however, are highly compatible with a cultural evolutionary account. Fertility decline followed cultural boundaries because reproductive norms were transmitted through culturally structured social networks.

The contrast between France and England and Wales illustrates this point. Industrialization alone did not determine the timing of fertility decline. Social modernization, secularization, political transformation, inheritance institutions, and changing systems of prestige shaped the cultural acceptance of fertility limitation.

The most provocative conclusion is that modern low fertility may be culturally successful while remaining biologically maladaptive. Cultural evolution does not necessarily maximize the reproductive success of the individuals carrying its traits. It favours ideas and behaviours that are effectively transmitted.

From this perspective, the demographic transition is not merely the final stage of economic development. It is an example of how human culture, operating through imitation, prestige, institutions, and communication, can redirect behaviour away from the reproductive outcomes favoured by genetic selection.

Culture gave human populations an extraordinary capacity to adapt rapidly to new environments. Yet the same capacity also allows cultural traits to spread for reasons unrelated to biological fitness. The demographic transition may be one of the clearest historical examples of this fundamental tension between cultural and genetic evolution.
