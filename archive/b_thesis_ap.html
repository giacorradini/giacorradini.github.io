Rethinking the Demographic Transition: my a-posteriory Thoughts, more than 10 Years Later

Gianmarco Corradini, 2026

More than ten years ago, I wrote my bachelor’s thesis on the demographic transition in modern Europe. The thesis compared the demographic histories of England and Wales with that of France, concentrating on the decline of mortality, economic modernization, secularization, and the diffusion of fertility control. It was a literature-based thesis: I reviewed existing explanations and historical evidence, but I did not conduct an original statistical analysis or develop an independent theoretical interpretation.

Looking back, I still consider the historical evidence presented in that thesis valuable. However, I now interpret it through a different theoretical framework. In particular, I believe that dual inheritance theory provides a more coherent explanation of the demographic transition than approaches based exclusively on economic incentives, mortality decline, or individual reproductive choice.

My present argument is that the demographic transition was fundamentally a process of cultural evolution. Economic and epidemiological changes created the conditions within which fertility could decline, but they do not fully explain why low-fertility behaviour emerged, spread across populations, and eventually became dominant. The decisive transformation concerned not only the material conditions of family life, but also the mechanisms through which people acquired values, aspirations, and models of successful behaviour.

The Limits of Conventional Explanations

The classical theory of demographic transition describes a movement from a demographic regime characterized by high mortality and high fertility to one characterized by low mortality and low fertility. Mortality normally declines first as nutrition, sanitation, medicine, and public health improve. Fertility then declines after a period of rapid population growth.

This description identifies an important historical sequence, but it does not by itself explain the decision to restrict fertility. A fall in infant mortality may reduce the number of births required to achieve a desired number of surviving children, but it does not determine what that desired number should be. Nor does it explain why fertility sometimes remained high for decades after mortality had already declined.

Economic theories provide a more explicit mechanism. Industrialization reduced the productive value of children, increased the importance of education, and raised the direct and indirect costs of childrearing. Children became less valuable as workers and more expensive as recipients of parental investment. At the same time, paid employment and professional advancement increased the opportunity cost of parenthood, particularly for women.

These arguments are persuasive, but they normally treat preferences as relatively stable. Individuals are assumed to continue pursuing the same objectives while adjusting their reproductive decisions to new costs, benefits, and constraints. Fertility declines because the economic environment changes.

The historical evidence, however, suggests that modernization also changed the preferences themselves. Ideas about marriage, parenthood, sexuality, personal fulfilment, consumption, and social status did not remain fixed while economic circumstances changed around them. They evolved and spread through increasingly powerful systems of cultural transmission.

The Inconclusive Legacy of the Princeton European Fertility Project

The Princeton European Fertility Project was one of the most ambitious attempts to study the historical decline of fertility. It reconstructed fertility patterns across more than six hundred European administrative units and documented substantial variation in the timing and speed of the transition (Coale and Watkins, 1986; Richerson and Boyd, 2005).

Its results weakened simple economic accounts of fertility decline. Fertility did not consistently begin to fall in the most industrialized regions. France experienced a remarkably early decline, beginning around 1830, although its economic modernization was slower than that of Britain and Germany. Britain and Germany entered the transition considerably later, while some German regions maintained high fertility into the early twentieth century (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, pp. 172–173).

At the same time, fertility decline frequently followed linguistic, religious, and cultural boundaries. French-speaking regions of Belgium entered the transition before Flemish-speaking regions. Catalonia preceded much of the rest of Spain, Hungary preceded other parts of the Austro-Hungarian Empire, and Brittany and Normandy entered the transition much later than most of France (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, p. 173).

These patterns indicated that fertility behaviour spread through culturally structured populations rather than simply following income, industrialization, or urbanization. The timing of fertility decline was connected to historically inherited systems of language, religion, social organization, and communication.

In this sense, the Princeton project was extremely important but theoretically inconclusive. It demonstrated that economic modernization alone could not explain the European fertility transition, but it was not designed to collect the fine-grained evidence required to identify the cultural processes responsible for the diffusion of fertility control (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, p. 173).

The project showed that culture mattered, but it did not possess a theory explaining how cultural traits are transmitted, why some ideas spread more effectively than others, or how cultural evolution can produce behaviour that conflicts with reproductive success. Dual inheritance theory provides a framework for addressing these questions.

Demographic Transition as Cultural Evolution

Dual inheritance theory begins from the idea that human behaviour is shaped by two interacting inheritance systems: genetic inheritance and cultural inheritance. Culture is not merely a collection of ideas placed on top of human biology. It is an inheritance system through which beliefs, preferences, practices, skills, and institutions are transmitted between individuals and across generations (Richerson and Boyd, 2005).

Genetic and cultural evolution do not necessarily favour the same outcomes. Genetic selection favours traits that increase biological reproduction. Cultural selection favours traits that are successfully learned, remembered, imitated, taught, or reproduced by institutions. A cultural trait can therefore spread even when it reduces the reproductive success of the individuals who adopt it.

In traditional agrarian societies, cultural and genetic interests were often relatively closely aligned. The family was the principal unit of production, consumption, protection, and socialization. Cultural transmission was predominantly vertical: children learned their values, occupations, and reproductive norms from parents and relatives.

Under these conditions, cultural norms commonly promoted marriage, parenthood, family continuity, and the accumulation of resources for descendants. Large and economically successful families could increase both social influence and biological fitness (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, pp. 170–171).

Modernization disrupted this alignment. Education, urbanization, bureaucratic employment, industrial production, literacy, and mass communication increased the importance of cultural influences originating outside the family. Teachers, employers, professional colleagues, political figures, writers, and entertainers became increasingly influential cultural models.

The demographic transition can therefore be interpreted partly as a transition from predominantly vertical cultural transmission, from parents to children, toward horizontal and oblique transmission through peers, institutions, prestigious individuals, and mass media (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, pp. 171–172).

Prestige and the Diffusion of Low Fertility

Humans do not evaluate every possible behaviour independently. We frequently use social information, and one important social-learning mechanism is prestige bias: the tendency to learn disproportionately from people who appear successful, respected, knowledgeable, or socially influential (Richerson and Boyd, 2005).

Modern societies created new forms of prestige. High status became increasingly associated with educational achievement, professional specialization, career advancement, urban lifestyles, cultural sophistication, and individual autonomy.

Success in these domains often required delayed marriage, geographical mobility, long periods of education, and substantial investment in professional competition. The individuals who achieved the greatest visibility within modern institutions were therefore disproportionately likely to model lifestyles characterized by delayed parenthood and smaller families.

Other people did not necessarily imitate reproductive behaviour in isolation. They imitated a broader package of values and aspirations: education, occupational success, consumption, independence, and personal development. Low fertility spread as one component of this culturally attractive package (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, pp. 176–179).

From this perspective, economic modernization affected fertility indirectly as well as directly. It created institutions in which people following low-fertility strategies could acquire prestige and exercise cultural influence. Industrialization did not merely make children more expensive; it also reorganized prestige, social learning, and the transmission of aspirations.

Why France Moved First

The contrast between France and England and Wales is especially revealing. England experienced earlier and more intensive industrialization, but France entered the fertility transition sooner. A strictly economic model has difficulty explaining this sequence.

France underwent an unusually early transformation of its social and ideological order. The Revolution weakened traditional authorities, challenged the institutional position of the Church, promoted legal equality, and strengthened individualistic conceptions of property and family interest.

The system of partible inheritance consolidated under the Napoleonic Code also encouraged property-owning families to consider the consequences of dividing their estates among numerous heirs. These institutions did not mechanically cause fertility decline, but they contributed to a cultural environment in which deliberate family limitation became more acceptable.

Fertility control became increasingly compatible with ideals of rational household management, individual autonomy, social mobility, and the preservation of family property. France may therefore be interpreted as a case in which social and cultural modernization moved ahead of industrial modernization.

England and Wales, by contrast, industrialized earlier while retaining reproductive norms that remained resistant to deliberate fertility limitation for a longer period. As Richerson and Boyd observe, social modernization could move ahead of industrial development, as in France, while industrialization could precede a slower cultural transformation, as in Britain (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, p. 187).

The different trajectories do not show that economics was irrelevant. Rather, economic change was filtered through historically specific cultural systems. Similar material pressures produced different demographic outcomes because populations differed in their inherited beliefs, institutions, social networks, and systems of prestige.

Culture Can Be Maladaptive

The most important implication of dual inheritance theory is that culture need not maximize biological fitness. This distinguishes a cultural evolutionary explanation from theories that interpret low fertility as an adaptive response to modern conditions.

One possible adaptive explanation is the trade-off between the quantity and quality of children. Parents may have fewer children because each child requires greater investment in education, health, and social development. Producing fewer but more successful children could theoretically produce a larger number of surviving descendants in later generations.

However, the evidence discussed by Richerson and Boyd provides little support for the claim that modern low fertility generally maximizes the number of grandchildren. Research comparing reproductive histories found that groups investing more resources in fewer children did not necessarily produce more grandchildren. Greater wealth and investment could coexist with lower long-term reproductive success (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, pp. 173–174).

The adaptive interpretation becomes still less convincing when fertility falls below replacement level. A reproductive strategy that systematically produces too few descendants to replace the parental generation cannot easily be described as biologically fitness maximizing.

Dual inheritance theory does not imply that individuals consciously reject biological reproduction or behave irrationally. Individuals may act coherently according to culturally acquired preferences. They may pursue education, professional recognition, consumption, autonomy, leisure, or personal fulfilment.

These objectives can be individually meaningful and socially rewarded while producing lower long-term genetic fitness. Cultural success and reproductive success are therefore different phenomena. A lifestyle can be highly visible, prestigious, and widely imitated while reducing the number of descendants produced by its adopters.

In this sense, low fertility may be culturally successful while remaining biologically maladaptive. Cultural variants are selected according to their ability to spread, not according to their contribution to the genetic fitness of the individuals who carry them (Richerson and Boyd, 2005).

Consumerism and Competing Aspirations

Modern economic development enormously expanded the range of activities competing with parenthood. Individuals could invest time and resources in education, careers, travel, hobbies, entertainment, consumption, and relationships outside the extended family.

These alternatives did not merely raise the financial cost of children. They changed the perceived meaning of a successful life. Parenthood ceased to be the only widely available route to adulthood, social status, identity, and recognition.

Consumer culture is particularly important because it continually generates new desires and activities. Rising income does not necessarily lead people to have more children once their basic needs have been satisfied. It can instead increase the number of goods, experiences, and lifestyles against which parenthood must compete (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, pp. 174–176).

The relevant scarcity is therefore not only money. It is also time, attention, identity, and commitment. Even affluent individuals may perceive children as incompatible with the lifestyles they have learned to value.

Material prosperity consequently need not restore high fertility. It may intensify competition between reproduction and a continually expanding set of culturally transmitted aspirations.

The Transformation of Women’s Social Roles

The expansion of female education was one of the most powerful elements of the demographic transition. Traditional societies generally restricted women’s access to prestigious roles outside the household. Motherhood and domestic competence were consequently central sources of status and identity.

Mass education exposed girls and women to alternative models of success. Schools transmitted knowledge, but they also transmitted aspirations. Educational achievement demonstrated that women could compete successfully for occupational and professional positions previously reserved primarily for men.

Schools also exposed girls to teachers and other individuals occupying prestigious modern roles. These models made employment, professional achievement, and participation in the modern economy appear attainable and socially desirable (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, p. 177).

As women gained access to these roles, the opportunity costs of early and repeated childbearing increased. More importantly, the cultural definition of female success changed. Marriage and motherhood became possible elements of a successful life rather than its exclusive foundation.

This development cannot be reduced to a mechanical calculation of wages and childcare costs. It represents a transformation in identity, prestige, and culturally transmitted preferences. Female education affected fertility because it reorganized both economic opportunities and the cultural models available to women.

Mass Communication and Accelerating Transitions

The European demographic transition developed through schools, cities, migration, political movements, religious change, newspapers, and interpersonal networks. Later transitions occurred in a world increasingly connected by radio, television, cinema, international organizations, and eventually digital media.

These communication technologies increased the speed at which fertility norms could spread. Populations no longer needed to reach the same level of industrial development experienced by nineteenth-century Europe before encountering modern models of family life.

Bongaarts and Watkins argue that development multiplies the channels through which local communities encounter new information and new reproductive norms. Education, migration, personal networks, government programmes, and mass media connect local populations to national and international cultural influences (Bongaarts and Watkins, 1996).

Media frequently portray attractive and prestigious individuals living urban, consumption-oriented lives in which children are absent, delayed, or few. Even without explicitly promoting fertility limitation, such representations communicate assumptions about what modernity and success look like (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, pp. 179–180).

The demographic transition can therefore begin at lower levels of economic development when communities become connected to national and global networks of cultural transmission. Ideas about contraception and family size spread not only because they are materially useful, but because they become associated with socially desirable identities.

Resistance as a Natural Experiment

High-fertility religious communities provide an important test of this interpretation. Groups such as the Amish, Hutterites, and some ultra-Orthodox Jewish communities participate in modern economies and benefit from modern medicine, yet have historically maintained much higher fertility than surrounding populations (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, pp. 180–186).

Their behaviour cannot be explained simply by poverty, economic underdevelopment, or lack of access to contraception. Their relatively high fertility is sustained by cultural institutions that protect pronatalist norms.

These communities restrict exposure to external media, maintain strong family structures, regulate education, create internal systems of prestige, and impose social costs on deviation. Individuals obtain much of their cultural information from parents, relatives, religious authorities, and community members rather than from the prestige hierarchy of the surrounding society.

The Amish and Hutterites are not completely isolated from the modern economy. They purchase and sell goods, employ modern productive technology, and benefit from modern medicine. Their separation is primarily maintained through selective cultural transmission rather than complete economic isolation.

Their existence therefore demonstrates that industrial production, prosperity, and low mortality do not automatically generate low fertility. Where communities successfully limit the diffusion of external fertility-reducing norms, high fertility can persist despite substantial economic modernization.

These communities function as a natural experiment. They support the argument that demographic transition depends not only on material conditions but also on the organization of cultural learning, conformity, education, prestige, and social interaction.

Reinterpreting the Demographic Transition

I would now reinterpret the demographic transition as the result of an interaction between material change and cultural evolution. Improvements in survival, industrialization, contraception, education, and urbanization were indispensable, but none of them operated independently of culture.

Mortality decline altered the demographic environment. Industrialization changed the economic role of children. Education expanded individual opportunities. Contraception made deliberate fertility control more reliable. Urbanization weakened traditional family structures. Mass communication increased exposure to alternative lifestyles.

Together, these changes transformed the cultural transmission system. Non-parental influences became stronger, prestigious social models became less family-centred, and reproductive behaviour became increasingly detached from the norms favoured by vertical transmission.

Low fertility spread because it was embedded in a broader cultural complex associated with modern success. This complex included education, individualism, professional ambition, consumption, autonomy, and the possibility of constructing an identity outside parenthood.

The demographic transition was therefore not simply a rational adjustment to new economic conditions. It was also the evolutionary diffusion of a new conception of the good life.

Conclusion

My original thesis concluded that the demographic transition resulted from the interaction of economic, epidemiological, and cultural forces. I still agree with that conclusion, but I would now assign culture a more central explanatory role.

Economic and epidemiological modernization changed the environment in which reproductive decisions were made. Dual inheritance theory explains how these transformations also altered the transmission and cultural selection of the preferences guiding those decisions.

The evidence collected by the Princeton European Fertility Project was inconclusive in a specific theoretical sense. It undermined simple economic explanations without providing a complete alternative mechanism. Its regional findings, however, are highly compatible with a cultural evolutionary account. Fertility decline followed cultural boundaries because reproductive norms were transmitted through culturally structured social networks.

The contrast between France and England and Wales illustrates this point. Industrialization alone did not determine the timing of fertility decline. Social modernization, secularization, political transformation, inheritance institutions, and changing systems of prestige shaped the cultural legitimacy of fertility limitation.

The most provocative conclusion is that modern low fertility may be culturally successful while remaining biologically maladaptive. Cultural evolution does not necessarily maximize the reproductive success of the individuals carrying its traits. It favours ideas and behaviours that are effectively transmitted.

From this perspective, demographic transition is not merely the final stage of economic development. It is an example of how human culture, operating through imitation, prestige, institutions, conformity, and communication, can redirect behaviour away from the reproductive outcomes favoured by genetic selection.

Culture gave human populations an extraordinary capacity to adapt rapidly to new environments. Yet this capacity entails an evolutionary trade-off: the mechanisms that permit useful cultural knowledge to spread can also permit the diffusion of cultural variants that reduce biological fitness (Richerson and Boyd, 2005, pp. 188–190).

The demographic transition may therefore be one of the clearest historical examples of the tension between cultural success and genetic reproduction. Culture is adaptive as a system, but its individual products are not necessarily adaptive for the people who adopt them.
