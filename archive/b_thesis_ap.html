Rethinking the Demographic Transition: my a-posteriory Thoughts, more than 10 Years Later

Gianmarco Corradini, 2026

More than ten years ago, I wrote my bachelor’s thesis on the demographic transition in modern Europe. It compared England and Wales with France and reviewed the effects of mortality decline, industrialization, secularization, and fertility control.

I still consider much of that historical evidence valuable, but I now interpret it differently. My central argument is that the demographic transition cannot be fully understood as an economically rational or biologically adaptive response to modernization. It is better understood as one of the clearest historical examples of maladaptive cultural evolution.

Economic development created the conditions in which fertility could decline. However, cultural evolution explains why low-fertility values and lifestyles spread, even when they reduced the biological fitness of the people who adopted them.

Why Conventional Explanations Are Incomplete

Classical demographic transition theory describes a movement from high mortality and high fertility to low mortality and low fertility. Mortality usually declines first because of improvements in nutrition, sanitation, medicine, and public health. Fertility then falls after a period of rapid population growth.

This sequence is historically important, but it does not fully explain reproductive behaviour. Lower infant mortality may reduce the number of births required to produce a certain number of surviving children, but it does not explain why parents should desire only one or two children. Nor does it explain why fertility sometimes remained high for decades after mortality had already fallen.

Economic theories provide a stronger mechanism. Industrialization reduced the productive value of children and increased the costs of education and childrearing. Employment and professional advancement also increased the opportunity cost of parenthood, especially for women.

These explanations are persuasive, but they usually assume that individual preferences remain stable. People are expected to pursue the same goals while adapting their reproductive decisions to new economic conditions. Yet modernization did not merely change the cost of children. It changed what people considered desirable, prestigious, and meaningful.

The Evidence from the Princeton Project

The Princeton European Fertility Project reconstructed fertility patterns across hundreds of European regions. Its findings weakened simple accounts based on industrialization and income because fertility did not consistently decline first in the most economically advanced areas (Coale and Watkins, 1986; Richerson and Boyd, 2005).

France entered the fertility transition before Britain and Germany despite industrializing later. Fertility decline also frequently followed linguistic, religious, and regional boundaries. French-speaking Belgium moved before Flemish-speaking Belgium, Catalonia before much of Spain, and several culturally distinctive regions resisted decline long after nearby populations had changed.

The project therefore demonstrated that culture mattered. However, it did not provide a complete theory of how reproductive norms spread. It showed that fertility decline moved through culturally structured populations, but it could not fully explain why some low-fertility ideas became successful.

Dual inheritance theory provides the missing mechanism.

Two Systems of Inheritance

Dual inheritance theory proposes that human behaviour is shaped by two interacting inheritance systems: genetic inheritance and cultural inheritance. Genetic information is transmitted biologically, while beliefs, values, preferences, practices, and institutions are transmitted socially (Richerson and Boyd, 2005).

These two systems do not necessarily favour the same outcomes. Genetic selection favours traits that contribute to biological reproduction. Cultural selection favours traits that are effectively learned, imitated, taught, remembered, and reproduced by institutions.

A cultural trait can therefore become widespread even when it lowers the reproductive success of the individuals who adopt it. Cultural success is not the same thing as biological success.

In agrarian societies, the two systems were often more closely aligned. The family was the principal unit of production, protection, socialization, and economic continuity. Children learned most of their values and expectations from parents and relatives. This form of vertical cultural transmission commonly supported marriage, parenthood, large families, and the transfer of resources to descendants.

Modernization weakened the dominance of the family as a source of cultural information. Schools, cities, workplaces, professional institutions, newspapers, television, and later digital media increased the importance of horizontal and oblique transmission. People increasingly learned from peers, teachers, employers, celebrities, professionals, and other prestigious individuals.

Prestige and Low Fertility

Humans often imitate people who appear successful, respected, competent, or influential. This tendency is known as prestige bias. Rather than evaluating every possible lifestyle independently, individuals use the apparent success of others as a guide.

Modern institutions created new forms of prestige. Status became increasingly associated with education, professional achievement, urban lifestyles, mobility, consumption, autonomy, and personal development.

Success in these areas often required long periods of education, delayed marriage, geographical mobility, and intense investment in professional competition. Highly visible and prestigious individuals were therefore disproportionately likely to model lifestyles based on late parenthood, limited family size, or childlessness.

People did not necessarily imitate low fertility directly. They imitated a broader cultural package: education, independence, professional ambition, consumption, travel, leisure, and self-development. Low fertility spread as part of this attractive model of modern success.

Industrialization therefore did more than make children expensive. It created institutions in which low-fertility lifestyles generated prestige and cultural influence.

Culture Can Be Maladaptive

This is the central point. Culture does not necessarily maximize biological fitness. Cultural traits are selected according to their capacity to spread, not according to the number of descendants produced by the people who adopt them.

Some theories interpret low fertility as an adaptive trade-off between the quantity and quality of children. Parents may have fewer children in order to invest more resources in each one. In theory, highly educated and successful children could later produce more descendants.

However, the evidence discussed by Richerson and Boyd provides little support for the idea that modern low fertility generally maximizes the number of grandchildren. Greater wealth and greater investment in children can coexist with lower long-term reproductive success (Richerson and Boyd, 2005).

The adaptive interpretation becomes even less convincing when fertility falls below replacement level. A reproductive pattern that systematically produces too few descendants to replace the parental generation cannot plausibly be described as maximizing biological fitness.

This does not mean that individuals are irrational. People may act coherently according to preferences they have acquired culturally. They may pursue education, professional recognition, autonomy, consumption, leisure, travel, or personal fulfilment.

These goals can be meaningful and socially rewarded while reducing reproductive success. A lifestyle may therefore be culturally prestigious, widely imitated, and institutionally supported while remaining biologically maladaptive.

Modern low fertility is not an accidental failure of cultural evolution. It is a predictable consequence of a cultural inheritance system whose selection criteria differ from those of genetic evolution.

Consumerism and Competing Aspirations

Modern economic development expanded the number of activities competing with parenthood. Individuals could invest increasing amounts of time, attention, and resources in education, careers, consumption, hobbies, relationships, entertainment, and travel.

These alternatives did not merely increase the financial cost of children. They transformed the cultural meaning of a successful life. Parenthood ceased to be the only widely recognized route to adulthood, identity, social status, and fulfilment.

Consumer culture is especially important because it continuously produces new desires. Higher income does not necessarily lead to higher fertility. It may instead expand the range of goods, experiences, and identities against which parenthood must compete.

The relevant scarcity is therefore not only money. It is also time, attention, commitment, and identity. Even affluent individuals may perceive children as incompatible with the lifestyle they have learned to value.

Women’s Education and New Models of Success

Female education was one of the strongest forces associated with fertility decline. Traditional societies restricted women’s access to prestigious roles outside the household, making marriage, motherhood, and domestic competence central sources of identity and status.

Mass education exposed women to alternative models of success. Schools transmitted not only knowledge but also aspirations. They demonstrated that professional employment, economic independence, and public achievement were attainable and socially valued.

This raised the opportunity cost of early and repeated childbearing, but the transformation was not merely economic. Education changed the cultural definition of a successful woman. Marriage and motherhood became possible components of a meaningful life rather than its exclusive foundation.

France and Britain

The contrast between France and Britain illustrates why industrialization alone is insufficient. Britain industrialized earlier, but France entered the fertility transition sooner.

France experienced an early transformation of its social and ideological order. The Revolution weakened traditional authorities, challenged the institutional position of the Church, promoted legal equality, and strengthened individualistic ideas about property and family interest.

Partible inheritance also encouraged property-owning families to consider the consequences of dividing estates among numerous heirs. These institutions did not mechanically produce fertility decline, but they made deliberate family limitation more culturally acceptable.

France can therefore be interpreted as a society in which cultural and social modernization moved ahead of industrial modernization. Britain, by contrast, industrialized earlier while retaining reproductive norms that remained resistant to deliberate fertility control for longer.

Resistance as a Natural Experiment

High-fertility religious communities provide further evidence that modernization does not automatically produce low fertility. Groups such as the Amish and Hutterites participate in modern economies and benefit from modern medicine, yet have historically maintained much higher fertility than surrounding populations.

Their fertility cannot be explained by poverty, ignorance, or the absence of modern technology. It is protected by cultural institutions that sustain pronatalist values.

These communities maintain strong family structures, distinctive systems of prestige, regulated education, and limited exposure to external media. Cultural information continues to flow primarily through parents, relatives, religious authorities, and community members.

Their existence demonstrates that prosperity, low mortality, and modern production do not inevitably produce low fertility. Fertility remains high where communities successfully protect vertical transmission and restrict the spread of external low-fertility norms.

Conclusion

The demographic transition resulted from an interaction between material change and cultural evolution. Mortality decline, industrialization, contraception, education, and urbanization created the conditions in which fertility could fall. Cultural evolution explains why low-fertility values became attractive, prestigious, and widely transmitted.

The most important conclusion is that culture can be maladaptive. Human culture evolved because it allowed populations to acquire and transmit useful information rapidly. However, the mechanisms that spread beneficial knowledge can also spread preferences and lifestyles that reduce biological fitness.

Modern low fertility may be individually rational and culturally successful while remaining reproductively maladaptive. Education, professional ambition, autonomy, consumption, and personal development can all increase social status while decreasing the number of descendants produced.

The demographic transition should therefore not be interpreted simply as the final stage of economic development. It is one of the clearest historical examples of cultural evolution diverging from genetic evolution.

Culture is adaptive as an inheritance system, but the individual traits it propagates need not be adaptive. Once cultural success became increasingly detached from reproductive success, below-replacement fertility became not only possible but predictable.
