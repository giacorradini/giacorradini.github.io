A few centimeters from tragedy: A psychological reflection on risk perception and avoidance

Last night, I experienced a severe hit-and-run incident while riding my scooter after dark. A driver blasted past me at an extreme speed, driving so close that they ripped off a piece of my vehicle before fleeing the scene. By some miracle, I managed to stay upright, but the psychological shock was immediate. Just a few centimeters to the left and the outcome would have been catastrophic—resulting in permanent paralysis, or worse, death.

Once the initial cognitive shock settled, it forced me into a deep reflection on human behavior. I have always been someone who experiences high levels of anxiety over minor, everyday issues—things most people naturally dismiss. This near-miss highlighted how completely warped our internal risk assessment can become. As humans, we are not algorithms; a significant gap between perceived risk and objective danger is a fundamental trait of our psychology. However, acute events like this completely shatter and recalibrate that entire measurement scale.

This experience served as a brutal but necessary tool for cognitive restructuring. It taught me the urgent need to retune my perception of danger and to stop draining mental energy on trivial complaints, especially in a world that is inherently unpredictable. More importantly, it reinforced a core psychological truth: avoidance is a trap. While hiding away from the threat feels safe, avoiding situations only breeds deeper anxiety. It generalizes the trigger stimulus, reinforces fear, and locks us into a cycle of maladaptive avoidance behaviors. Life is fragile, and the best response to objective danger is not withdrawal, but a conscious, calibrated return to the road ahead.
